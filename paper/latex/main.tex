\begin{abstract}
Underwater corner reflectors are widely used as passive, high-contrast markers for marine detection and localization. However, dedicated deployment platforms specifically designed for underwater corner reflectors are rarely reported in the open literature. This paper presents a mission-oriented, self-suspending underwater platform for corner-reflector deployment and characterizes the mission-defining transition from a compact near-horizontal release with nonzero initial velocity to a stable near-vertical working posture.

To characterize this transitional phase, we develop an interpretable planar three-degree-of-freedom (3-DOF) dynamic model encompassing surge, heave, and pitch. The directional coupling between the rigid-body motion and the entrained internal water within the platform's open-frame structure is captured by three bounded, anisotropic permeability-corrected inertial parameters ($\mu_x$, $\mu_z$, and $\mu_\theta$). To achieve hydrodynamic closure, static forces and moments are mapped using CFD-derived coefficient tables, whereas rotational damping and the remaining dynamic parameters are identified through standardized free-decay experiments, employing strict condition-level data splitting to separate calibration from validation.

With the identified parameters strictly locked, the integrated dynamic model accurately reproduces independent out-of-sample free-decay trajectories without any parameter retuning. Quantitative validation across these independent conditions yields an $\mathrm{RMSE}_\theta$ of $\langle TBD\rangle$ deg and an $\mathrm{RMSE}_q$ of $\langle TBD\rangle$ rad/s, accompanied by a settling-time mismatch of only $\langle TBD\rangle$ s and an overshoot error of $\langle TBD\rangle$ deg. Leveraging this validated model, subsequent parametric analyses delineate a precise initial-condition feasibility envelope. This ultimately provides bounded, sensitivity-based operational guidance tailored specifically to the present platform configuration during its critical horizontal-to-vertical transition phase.
\end{abstract}
