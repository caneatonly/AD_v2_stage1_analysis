# Abstract

Underwater corner reflectors are widely studied as passive markers/targets for marine sensing and localization, yet dedicated open-literature reports on miniature deployment platforms for corner-reflector placement are limited. This paper reports the design and characterization of a mission-oriented self-suspending underwater platform developed for corner-reflector deployment, and treats the platform architecture, mass--buoyancy arrangement, and deployment workflow as primary engineering contributions.

For the platform considered here, the mission-critical phase is a transition from a compact near-horizontal release with nonzero initial velocity to a near-vertical working posture. To analyze and guide this phase, we develop an interpretable planar three-degree-of-freedom dynamic model in surge, heave, and pitch. Directional inner-water coupling is represented through anisotropic permeability-corrected inertial terms with three bounded parameters, \(\mu_x\), \(\mu_z\), and \(\mu_{\theta}\). Static hydrodynamic loads are closed using CFD-derived coefficient mappings as a controlled replacement of static force and moment terms, while rotational damping and remaining dynamic parameters are identified from protocolized free-decay experiments under condition-aware split governance.

The resulting platform--model package is validated against free-decay experiments without retuning and is then used to derive sensitivity-based operating guidance for the current configuration. All claims are explicitly limited to the studied platform geometry, mass distribution, buoyancy layout, and actuation strategy, and to the horizontal-launch to vertical-stabilization phase.
