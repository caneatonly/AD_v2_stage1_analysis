# Abstract

Reliable underwater corner-reflector deployment depends not only on payload packaging but also on the transition dynamics of the carrier platform after release. This paper presents the design and characterization of a mission-oriented self-suspending underwater platform developed specifically for corner-reflector deployment, with emphasis on the horizontal-launch to vertical-stabilization phase that determines whether the platform can reach its intended working posture. In this study, platform architecture, mass-buoyancy arrangement, and deployment workflow are treated as primary engineering contributions rather than background conditions.

To characterize this transition, we formulate an anisotropic permeability-corrected three-degree-of-freedom dynamic model in surge, heave, and pitch. The model introduces three physically interpretable coupling parameters, \(\mu_x\), \(\mu_z\), and \(\mu_{\theta}\), to represent directional inner-water coupling effects. Static CFD-derived hydrodynamic mappings are used to close the force and moment model and to support mechanism interpretation, whereas rotational damping is retained as an experimentally identified term. A reproducible evidence chain is established through protocolized free-decay preprocessing and segmentation, condition-level split control, parameter identification, simulation-to-experiment validation, and sensitivity-based design analysis.

The resulting platform-model framework is designed to support both physical interpretation and deployment-oriented engineering decisions under bounded assumptions. All conclusions are explicitly restricted to the current platform geometry, mass distribution, and actuation strategy, and the manuscript is organized to separate model construction, validation evidence, and design guidance for transparent future extension.
