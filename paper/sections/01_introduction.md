Underwater corner reflectors have emerged as pivotal passive, high-contrast markers for marine detection and localization(Luo et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2022). Their practical effectiveness, however, relies not only on the intrinsic acoustic performance of the reflectors but also on whether they can be deployed reliably into the target underwater environment. In real-world marine operations, the stringent spatial constraints of host vehicles often preclude the direct release of reflectors in their fully expanded working configurations. Instead, the reflector system must first be compactly stowed and then deployed only after the carrier reaches an appropriate release condition. As a result, the overall deployment reliability is governed not only by the payload itself but also by the structural design and post-release transitional dynamics of the carrier platform(Wang et al., 2024). Developing a mission-oriented underwater deployment platform is therefore essential for mitigating deployment risks and maximizing operational effectiveness. 
Existing studies related to the present problem can be broadly categorized into two groups: payload-oriented acoustic investigations and platform-oriented hydrodynamic studies. The former mainly focus on how reflector geometry, internal medium, and configuration affect target strength and scattering directivity. Representative efforts include air-cavity-based enhancement designs, array-configuration analyses, and recent experimental studies on flexible inflatable reflector structures (Xiao et al., 2025; Zhou et al., 2025). These works have substantially advanced the understanding of the acoustic behavior of underwater corner reflectors and, in some cases, have also considered compact storage and post-deployment performance. Overall, however, deployment is still largely treated as an external prerequisite, without further addressing a key issue of how a stowed reflector–carrier system should be designed and realized, and through what hydrodynamic process it can transition to its final working posture. In contrast, platform-oriented studies have focused more on vehicle-level hydrodynamic characteristics, CFD-assisted parameter extraction, and reduced-order dynamic modeling. The Classical Fossen framework remains the standard foundation for underwater vehicle dynamics modeling (Fossen ,2011). Meanwhile, CFD and experimental studies on open-frame or permeable platforms have shown that flow penetration, internal-water participation, and fluid–structure interaction can substantially alter added-mass and damping characteristics, rendering conventional closed-body assumptions inadequate for such configurations (Li et al., 2020; Molin, 2011). Free-decay identification provides a practical route for estimating low-speed hydrodynamic coefficients (Ross et al., 2004), while recent studies on separation safety further indicate that initial release conditions can strongly govern the evolution of the early transient response in underwater deployment systems (Wang et al., 2024). Nevertheless, existing platform studies have mostly addressed generic control, hovering operations, or safe separation, rather than the specific transitional dynamics of a mission-oriented miniature carrier that must start from a compact release state, undergo a large-attitude transition, stabilize into a near-vertical working posture, and subsequently release and deploy the corner reflector for operation. Therefore, a clear and important gap still remains between reflector acoustic design and deployment-stage dynamic modeling for underwater corner-reflector deployment systems.
Two key insights emerge from the aforementioned literature. First, a reduced-order model, supported by CFD and standardized experiments, provides a practical and efficient basis for evaluating complex transitional phases, provided its hydrodynamic closure is consistently formulated. Second, open-frame or permeable structures exhibit additional inertia-related mechanisms due to internal water entrainment and momentum exchange, which are highly dependent on the geometry and maneuver direction. However, a critical gap remains: modeling the large-attitude transition of a mission-oriented miniature platform introduces unique coupled challenges. Physically, the open structure required by packaging constraints yields strong, direction-dependent inertia coupling. Methodologically, accurately capturing this phenomenon requires seamlessly integrating CFD-based static closures with experimentally identified damping parameters, while strictly avoiding the double-counting of hydrodynamic terms.
To address the above gap, this study presents a mission-oriented miniature underwater platform for corner-reflector deployment and establishes a reduced-order dynamic framework for its transition-stage motion. The platform is designed to satisfy the coupled requirements of compact storage, post-release autonomous reorientation, and subsequent deployment operation. To characterize the critical horizontal-to-vertical transition, an interpretable planar three-degree-of-freedom (3-DOF) model in surge, heave, and pitch is developed. The directional coupling between rigid-body motion and internal fluid entrainment in the open-frame configuration is represented by three bounded anisotropic permeability-corrected inertial parameters, \mu_x, \mu_z, and \mu_\theta. Static hydrodynamic forces and moments are closed through CFD-derived coefficient mappings, so that geometry- and attitude-dependent quasi-static loads are incorporated explicitly without resorting to a fully CFD-resolved transient model. This treatment also prevents these contributions from being absorbed into the identification of dynamic terms, thereby reducing parameter confounding and avoiding double-counting. Rotational damping and other key dynamic terms are then identified from standardized free-decay experiments. To assess predictive capability under deployment-relevant conditions, a strict condition-level data-splitting strategy is adopted to separate calibration from out-of-sample validation. On this basis, sensitivity and parametric analyses are further conducted to delineate the feasible initial-condition range for successful transition, stabilization, and reflector deployment. Through this integrated hardware-model-experiment framework, the present study aims to bridge the gap between underwater corner-reflector payload design and deployment-stage dynamic modeling.
The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
	A mission-oriented, self-suspending miniature underwater platform is designed and fabricated for underwater corner-reflector deployment, providing a realizable carrier solution for compact-release and post-release stabilization tasks.
	A planar 3-DOF dynamic model is developed for the horizontal-to-vertical transition, in which anisotropic permeability-corrected inertial parameters are introduced to capture direction-dependent internal-water coupling effects in a bounded and physically interpretable manner.
	CFD-derived static force and moment mappings are integrated with free-decay-based parameter identification to form a practically identifiable reduced-order model, and strict out-of-sample validation is used to evaluate predictive performance under representative release conditions.
	Sensitivity-based analyses are performed to determine the feasible initial-condition envelope for successful transition, providing practical guidance for deployment design and operation.
